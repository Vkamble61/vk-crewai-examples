  • Type 1 diabetes reversed in landmark study, paving the way for human trials

    A groundbreaking study from Stanford School of Medicine revealed a potential cure for Type 1 diabetes in mice. Researchers reset the immune system and created new insulin-producing cells, curing all 19 pre-diabetic mice involved. This innovative approach could lead to revolutionary treatments for autoimmune diseases in humans.

    This finding not only opens the door for potential cures for autoimmune diseases in humans but also offers hope to millions with Type 1 diabetes for a future without dependence on insulin therapy.

  • Stanford's new cell therapy cures type 1 diabetes in mice

    Researchers at Stanford University developed a pioneering therapy involving blood stem cell and islet cell transplants that effectively cured mice of Type 1 diabetes by creating a hybrid immune system. This method may soon be applicable in human trials, potentially transforming treatment for Type 1 and other autoimmune diseases.

    The approach could signify a major step forward in treating Type 1 diabetes, transforming the management of the disease and enhancing the quality of life for patients.

    Recent research has identified critical lymph nodes in the pancreas and spleen that may be pivotal in preventing or detecting Type 1 diabetes. This could enhance early diagnosis and lead to innovative preventative measures.

    The discovery could revolutionize early intervention strategies for at-risk populations and significantly reduce the incidence of Type 1 diabetes.

  • Oral GLP-1 pill shows strong weight loss and blood sugar benefits in adults with diabetes

    A clinical trial demonstrated that a new oral GLP-1 medication significantly improved weight loss and blood sugar control in adults with Type 2 diabetes compared to a placebo, showcasing a promising advancement in diabetes management.

    This advancement provides a promising new management option for people with Type 2 diabetes, moving beyond injectable therapies and adapting treatment to patient preferences.

  • After 60 Years, Diabetes Drug Found to Unexpectedly Affect The Brain

    Researchers from Baylor College of Medicine discovered that Metformin, a common diabetes medication, works directly in the brain's hypothalamus, suggesting new mechanisms for its effectiveness and potential for broader applications in treatment.

    This discovery underscores the multifaceted impacts of existing diabetes treatments and suggests new avenues for addressing complications associated with diabetes.

  • Finerenone shows positive results for chronic kidney disease associated with T1D

    Bayer's phase 3 FINE-ONE trial indicated that Finerenone significantly decreased the level of albumin in urine, a marker for kidney damage in Type 1 diabetes patients. This could lead to a new treatment paradigm for managing renal complications in T1D.

    Given that kidney complications are a significant concern for Type 1 diabetes patients, this treatment could greatly enhance patient care and outcomes.
